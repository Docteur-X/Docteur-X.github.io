Proof by Induction

Method

A statement is basically a sentence that is either true or false. It might be a sentence, or an equation or a theorem or a fact, but it must be something objective that is definitively true or false.

Some examples of statements are: "My house is blue", "It is raining right now", "I have 10 dollars in my pocket". "3 plus 2 is 5" is a true statement, while "3 plus 2 is 6" is a false statement. [See the website on logic for more explanations of statements.]

Here are some statements that depend on a variable \(n\). "\(n\) is an even number" depends on what \(n\) is. This statement is true when \(n=2\) or \(n=4\) or \( n=6 \), and false when \(n=1\) or \(n=3\). We might abbreviate "\(n\) is an even number" into \( \textcolor{purple}{P}(n) \), so we don't have to keep on copying a long sentence again and again. Also, another notation, \( \textcolor{purple}{P}(n=3) \) or \( \textcolor{purple}{P}(3) \) represents the statement when \(n=3\), which is "\(n=3\) is an even number". So, \( \textcolor{purple}{P}(3) \) is false.

Here, one starts with a statement \( \textcolor{purple}{P}(n) \). Maybe we do a maths check and found out that \( \textcolor{purple}{P}(n) \) is true at \(n=\textcolor{blue}{1},\textcolor{orange}{2},\text{ and } \textcolor{green}{3}\) and we suspect that \( \textcolor{purple}{P}(n) \) is true for any whole number \( n \).

There's a slick way to do this. First check that \(\textcolor{purple}{P}(\textcolor{blue}{1})\) is true.

We then do a separate calculation. Use a placeholder letter \( k \) that can stand for any positive whole number. We then hope to show that if \( \textcolor{purple}{P}(k) \) is true, then \( \textcolor{purple}{P}(k+1) \) is true for all \( k \in \mathbb{Z} \). Let's pretend we've done it.

So, if \( P(k=11) \) is true (which it is), then \( P(k+1=2) \) is true. If \( P(k=2) \) is true (which is also true because of the previous explanation), then \( P(k+1=3) \) is true. If \( P(k=3) \) is true, so is \( P(k+1=4) \). If we keep on going, we get that \( P(n) \) is true for n=1,2,3,4... which is what we want to check for.

Examples to Convince You that it Works

Someone made a bold claim. For any number n, they say that the below statement is correct.

\[ \sum_{r=1}^n r^3 = \frac{1}{4}n^2(n+1)^2 \]

(In a clickable dropdown for the sum) \[ \sum_{r=1}^n r^3 \] means \[ 1^3 + 2^3 + 3^3 + \dots + n^3 \]. Add ref link to topic on summation.

Is the statement correct for \( \textcolor{blue}{n=1} \)? If you calculate the left-hand side and the right-hand side, you'll find that the statement is correct for \( \textcolor{blue}{n=1} \). Click to show working.

The skills needed to evaluate the left-hand side are just knowing what summation means and some arithmetics. The right-hand side is even easier, just plug in \( \textcolor{blue}{n=1} \) and calculate.

LHS: \( \displaystyle \sum_{r=1}^{\textcolor{blue}{n}} r^3 = \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{blue}{1} r^3 = 1^3 = 1 \)

RHS: \( \displaystyle \frac{1}{4}\textcolor{blue}{n}^2(\textcolor{blue}{n}+1)^2 = \frac{1}{4} \cdot \textcolor{blue}{1}^2 \cdot (\textcolor{blue}{1}+1)^2 = \frac{1}{4} \cdot 1 \cdot 2^2 = \frac{1}{4} \cdot 4 = 1 \)

Is it correct for \( \textcolor{orange}{n=2} \)? Let's check.

LHS: \( \displaystyle \sum_{r=1}^{\textcolor{orange}{n}} r^3 = \sum_{r=2}^\textcolor{orange}{1} r^3 = 1^3 + 2^3 = 1 + 8 = 9 \)

RHS: \( \displaystyle \frac{1}{4}\textcolor{orange}{n}^2(\textcolor{orange}{n}+1)^2 = \frac{1}{4} \cdot \textcolor{orange}{2}^2 \cdot (\textcolor{orange}{2}+1)^2 = \frac{1}{4} \cdot 2^2 \cdot 3^2 = \frac{1}{4} \cdot 4 \cdot 9 = 9 \)

So the statement is correct for \( \textcolor{orange}{n=2} \).

We can also check that the statement is correct for \( \textcolor{green}{n=3} \). And to check if the statement is correct for \( \textcolor{red}{n=4} \), we again substitute \( \textcolor{red}{n=4} \) into the equation and see if the left-hand side equals the right-hand side. We might enlist some slaves to help us tediously check if the statement is true for \(n=5, n=6, n=7, \dots\) or we can use some cleverer method.

A Better Way, which is why we learn Proof by Induction

I've shown that the statement \( \displaystyle \sum_{r=1}^n r^3 = \frac{1}{4}n^2(n+1)^2\) is true when \( \textcolor{orange}{n=2} \). I don't yet know if the statement is correct for \( \textcolor{green}{n=3} \). Can I use the statement at \( \textcolor{orange}{n=2} \) to show that the statement is true at \( \textcolor{green}{n=3} \)?

\[ \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{green}{3} r^3 \overset{?}{=} \frac{1}{4} \cdot \textcolor{green}{3}^2 \cdot (\textcolor{green}{3}+1)^2 \]

LHS: \[ \begin{aligned} \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{green}{3} r^3 &= \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{orange}{2} r^3 + \textcolor{green}{3}^3 & \textcolor{gray}{\text{Split off the last term from the summation}} \\ &= \frac{1}{4} \cdot \textcolor{orange}{2}^2 \cdot (\textcolor{orange}{2}+1)^2 + (\textcolor{orange}{2}+1)^3 & \textcolor{gray}{\text{Use statement at } n=2; \text{3 = 2+1}} \\ &= \frac{1}{4} \cdot (\textcolor{orange}{2}+1)^2 \cdot ( \textcolor{orange}{2}^2 + 4(\textcolor{orange}{2}+1)) & \textcolor{gray}{\text{Factor out } \frac{1}{4} \cdot (2+1)^2 \text{}} \\ &= \frac{1}{4} \cdot (\textcolor{orange}{2}+1)^2 \cdot ( \textcolor{orange}{2}^2 + 4 \cdot \textcolor{orange}{2}+4 ) ) & \textcolor{gray}{\text{Expand out 4(2+1)}} \\ &= \frac{1}{4} \cdot (\textcolor{orange}{2}+1)^2 \cdot (\textcolor{orange}{2}+2)^2 & \textcolor{gray}{\text{Use the fact that } (x+2)^2 = x^2 + 4x + 4, x = }\textcolor{orange}{2} \\ &= \frac{1}{4} \cdot (\textcolor{orange}{2}+1)^2 \cdot ( (\textcolor{orange}{2}+1) + 1 )^2 & \textcolor{gray}{\text{}} \\ &= \frac{1}{4} \cdot \textcolor{green}{3}^2 \cdot (\textcolor{green}{3} + 1)^2 & \textcolor{gray}{3 = 2+1} \\ &= \text{RHS} & \\ \end{aligned} \]

Thus, the statement is correct for \( \textcolor{green}{n=3} \). We've used the fact that the statement is true for \( \textcolor{orange}{n=2} \) in line 2.

Is the statement correct for \( \textcolor{red}{n=4} \)?

\[ \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{red}{4} r^3 \overset{?}{=} \frac{1}{4} \cdot \textcolor{red}{4}^2 \cdot (\textcolor{red}{4}+1)^2 \]

LHS: \[ \begin{aligned} \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{red}{4} r^3 &= \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{green}{3} r^3 + \textcolor{red}{4}^3 & \textcolor{gray}{\text{Split off the last term from the summation}} \\ &= \frac{1}{4} \cdot \textcolor{green}{3}^2 \cdot (\textcolor{green}{3}+1)^2 + (\textcolor{green}{3}+1)^3 & \textcolor{gray}{\text{Use statement at } n=3; \text{4 = 3+1}} \\ &= \frac{1}{4} \cdot (\textcolor{green}{3}+1)^2 \cdot ( \textcolor{green}{3}^2 + 4(\textcolor{green}{3}+1)) & \textcolor{gray}{\text{Factor out } \frac{1}{4} \cdot (3+1)^2 } \\ &= \frac{1}{4} \cdot (\textcolor{green}{3}+1)^2 \cdot ( \textcolor{green}{3}^2 + 4 \cdot \textcolor{green}{3}+4 ) ) & \textcolor{gray}{\text{Expand out 4(3+1)}} \\ &= \frac{1}{4} \cdot (\textcolor{green}{3}+1)^2 \cdot (\textcolor{green}{3}+2)^2 & \textcolor{gray}{\text{Use the fact that } (x+2)^2 = x^2 + 4x + 4, x = }\textcolor{green}{3} \\ &= \frac{1}{4} \cdot (\textcolor{green}{3}+1)^2 \cdot ( (\textcolor{green}{3}+1) + 1 )^2 & \textcolor{gray}{\text{}} \\ &= \frac{1}{4} \cdot \textcolor{red}{4}^2 \cdot (\textcolor{red}{4} + 1)^2 & \textcolor{gray}{4 = 3+1} \\ &= \text{RHS} & \\ \end{aligned} \]

Thus the statement is correct for \( \textcolor{red}{n=4} \).

Hopefully you see the trick here. You know that the statement is correct for \( n = \textcolor{blue}{1},\textcolor{orange}{2},\textcolor{green}{3},\textcolor{red}{4} \). Then do the following algebra:

\[ \begin{aligned} \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{purple}{n+1} r^3 &= \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{brown}{n} r^3 + (\textcolor{purple}{n+1})^3 \\ &= \frac{1}{4} \textcolor{brown}{n}^2(\textcolor{brown}{n}+1)^2 + (\textcolor{brown}{n}+1)^3 \\ &= \frac{1}{4} (\textcolor{brown}{n}+1)^2 ( \textcolor{brown}{n}^2 + 4(\textcolor{brown}{n}+1)) \\ &= \frac{1}{4} (\textcolor{brown}{n}+1)^2 ( \textcolor{brown}{n}^2 + 4\textcolor{brown}{n}+4)) \\ &= \frac{1}{4}(\textcolor{brown}{n}+1)^2(\textcolor{brown}{n}+2)^2\\ &= \frac{1}{4}(\textcolor{purple}{n+1})^2(\textcolor{purple}{n+1} + 1)^2 \end{aligned} \]

If the statement is correct for some \( \textcolor{brown}{n} \), the statement is correct for \( \textcolor{purple}{n+1} \). Try substituting \( \textcolor{orange}{n=2} \) or \( \textcolor{green}{n=3} \) and you will get the working that is shown above, and hopefully you understand why I did some weird manipulations for \(\textcolor{orange}{n=2}\) and \( \textcolor{green}{n=3} \). It's because the working is set up in a way so that it can be applied to any arbitrary \(n\)!

So, because the statement is correct for \(n=4\), it is correct for \(n=4+1=5\), and correct for \(n=5+1=6\), and correct for \(n=6+1=7\), and correct for \(n=8\), and \(n=9\), and ...

So the statement is correct for every positive integer.

To recap, we need to check that the statement is correct for \( \textcolor{blue}{n=1}\). Hopefully the statement is correct when \( \textcolor{blue}{n=1} \). Then, use algebra to show that if the statement is true for \( \textcolor{blue}{n=1} \), the statement is also true for \( \textcolor{orange}{n=2} \). The same algebra says that if the statement is true for \( \textcolor{orange}{n=2} \), the statement is also true for \( \textcolor{green}{n=3} \). And if the statement is true for \( \textcolor{green}{n=3} \), it's true for \( \textcolor{red}{n=4} \), and then \( \textcolor{purple}{n=5} \), and \( \textcolor{brown}{n=6} \), and ... This means that the statement is true for n=1,2,3,4,... which is what we want to show.

Another Summation Example

Someone else made another bold claim. They claim that the following statement is true for every positive integer n:

\[ \sum_{r=1}^n r(r+1) = \frac{n(n+1)(n+2)}{3} \]

Let's apply the procedure we've seen in the previous example.

Step 1. Check the statement is true for \( \textcolor{blue}{n=1} \).

LHS: \( \displaystyle \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{blue}{n} r(r+1) = \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{blue}{1} r(r+1) = \textcolor{blue}{1} \cdot (\textcolor{blue}{1} + 1) = 1 \cdot 2 = 2\)

RHS: \( \displaystyle \frac{\textcolor{blue}{n}(\textcolor{blue}{n}+1)(\textcolor{blue}{n}+2)}{3} = \frac{\textcolor{blue}{1}(\textcolor{blue}{1}+1)(\textcolor{blue}{1}+2)}{3} = \frac{1\cdot 2\cdot \cancel{3}}{\cancel{3}} = 2 \)

Thus the statement is true for \( \textcolor{blue}{n=1}\).

Step 2: Assume that \( \displaystyle \sum_{r=1}^n r(r+1) = \frac{n(n+1)(n+2)}{3} \) for some n. We know it is true for \( \textcolor{blue}{n=1}\); it might be true for other values of n as well. We'd like to show that \( \displaystyle \sum_{r=1}^{n+1} r(r+1) = \frac{(n+1)(n+2)(n+3)}{3} \).

\[ \begin{aligned} \sum_{r=1}^{\textcolor{purple}{n+1}} r(r+1) &= \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{brown}{n} r(r+1) + (\textcolor{purple}{n+1})((\textcolor{purple}{n+1})+1) & \textcolor{gray}{ \text{Split off the last term from the summation} } \\ &= \frac{\textcolor{brown}{n}(\textcolor{brown}{n}+1)(\textcolor{brown}{n}+2)}{3} + (\textcolor{brown}{n}+1)(\textcolor{brown}{n}+2) & \textcolor{gray}{ \text{Use statement at } } \textcolor{brown}{n} \\ &= \frac{(\textcolor{brown}{n}+1)(\textcolor{brown}{n}+2)}{3} \cdot (\textcolor{brown}{n} + 3) & \textcolor{gray}{ \text{Factor out } \frac{(n+1)(n+2)}{3} } \\ &= \frac{(\textcolor{brown}{n}+1)(\textcolor{brown}{n}+2)(\textcolor{brown}{n}+3)}{3} & \textcolor{gray}{ \text{Rearrange the factors that're multiplied together} } \\ &= \frac{(\textcolor{purple}{n+1})(\textcolor{purple}{n+1}+1)(\textcolor{purple}{n+1}+2)}{3} & \textcolor{gray}{ } \\ \end{aligned} \]

Thus, the statement is true for \( \textcolor{blue}{n=1} \), and is true for \( \textcolor{orange}{n=2} \), and true for \( \textcolor{green}{n=3} \), and \( \textcolor{red}{n=4} \), and ...

Proof of a statement related to Divisibility using Induction

Another person made another bold claim:

"The value of \( \displaystyle 3^{2n} + 11 \) calculated at any (positive integer) \(n\) is a whole number that is divisible by 4."

(Dropdown: If you have learned modular arithmetic, there is an easy proof.) \[ 3^{2n} + 11 \pmod{4} = (-1)^{2n} + 11 - 12 \pmod{4} = 1 + 11 - 12 \pmod{4} = 0 \pmod{4} \]

Step 1. Check the statement is true for \( \textcolor{blue}{n=1}\).

\[ \displaystyle 3^{2\textcolor{blue}{n}} + 11 = 3^{2 \textcolor{blue}{1}} + 11 = 3^2 + 11 = 9 + 11 = 20 = 4 \cdot 5\]

Thus, \( \displaystyle 3^{2n} + 11\) is divisible by 4 when \( \textcolor{blue}{n=1}\). So the statement is true for \( \textcolor{blue}{n=1}\)

Step 2. Show that if the statement is true at \(n\) (i.e. \( P(n) \) is true), then the statement is true at \( n+1 \) (i.e. \( P(n+1) \) is true).

\[ \begin{align} 3^{2(n+1)} + 11 &= 3^{2n+2} + 11 \\ &= 3^{2n} \cdot 3^2 + 11 \\ &= 3^{2n} \cdot 9 + 11 \\ &= 3^{2n} \cdot (8 + 1) + 11 \\ &= 3^{2n} \cdot 8 + 3^{2n} + 11 \\ &= \underbrace{ (3^{2n} \cdot 2) \cdot 4 }_\text{Multiple of 4} + \underbrace{(3^{2n} + 11)}_\text{Multiple of 4} \\ &= \text{Multiple of 4} \end{align} \]

Thus, if \( \displaystyle P(n) = " 3^{2n} + 11 \text{ is divisible by 4} " \) is a true statement, then \( \displaystyle P(n+1) = " 3^{2(n+1)} + 11 \text{ is divisible by 4} " \) is a true statement.

Then you can argue that because \( P(1) \) is true, then \( P(2) \) is true. If \( P(2) \) is true, then \( P(3) \) is true. If \( P(3) \) is true, then \( P(4) \) is true. And so on.

Proof of a Statement related to Matrices using Induction

\[ \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -4 \\ 1 & -1 \\ \end{pmatrix}^n = \begin{pmatrix} 2n+1 & -4n \\ n & 1-2n \\ \end{pmatrix} \] is true for all positive integers n.

\( \displaystyle \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -4 \\ 1 & -1 \\ \end{pmatrix}^n = \underbrace{\begin{pmatrix} 3 & -4 \\ 1 & -1 \\ \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -4 \\ 1 & -1 \\ \end{pmatrix} \dots \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -4 \\ 1 & -1 \\ \end{pmatrix}}_{n \text{ times}} \) in case you're wondering.

Step 1. Check the statement is true for \( \textcolor{blue}{n=1}\).

LHS = \( \displaystyle \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -4 \\ 1 & -1 \\ \end{pmatrix} ^ \textcolor{blue}{1} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -4 \\ 1 & -1 \\ \end{pmatrix} \)

RHS = \( \displaystyle \begin{pmatrix} 2 \textcolor{blue}{n} + 1 & - 4 \textcolor{blue}{n} \\ \textcolor{blue}{n} & 1-2 \textcolor{blue}{n} \\ \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \cdot \textcolor{blue}{1} + 1 & - 4 \cdot \textcolor{blue}{1} \\ \textcolor{blue}{1} & 1-2 \cdot \textcolor{blue}{1} \\ \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & - 4 \\ 1 & -1 \\ \end{pmatrix} \)

Step 2. Show that if \( P(n) \) is true, then \( P(n+1) \) is true.

\[ \begin{align} \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -4 \\ 1 & -1 \\ \end{pmatrix}^{n+1} &= \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -4 \\ 1 & -1 \\ \end{pmatrix}^{n} \cdot \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -4 \\ 1 & -1 \\ \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} 2n+1 & -4n \\ n & 1-2n \\ \end{pmatrix} \cdot \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -4 \\ 1 & -1 \\ \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} (2n+1) \cdot 3 + (-4n) \cdot 1 & (2n+1) \cdot -4 + (-4n) \cdot -1 \\ n \cdot 3 + (1-2n) \cdot 1 & n \cdot -4 + (1-2n) \cdot -1 \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} 6n + 3 - 4n & -8n - 4 + 4n \\ 3n + 1 - 2n & -4n - 1 + 2n \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} 2n+3 & -4n - 4 \\ n + 1 & - 2n - 1 \end{pmatrix} \\ \end{align} \]

It's relatively straightforwards to find \( P(n+1) \) so let's do that instead of continuing with factoring the algebra above to get the right form, which isn't super obvious. \( P(n+1) \) is the statement " \( \displaystyle \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -4 \\ 1 & -1 \\ \end{pmatrix}^{n+1} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 (n+1) +1 & -4 (n+1) \\ (n+1) & 1-2(n+1) \\ \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2n+3 & -4n-4 \\ n+1 & -1-2n \\ \end{pmatrix} \) " If you like, you can add this to the end of the above working in reverse order and actually get the \( P(n+1) \) statement.

Thus, we see that if we assume \( P(n) \) is true, then \( P(n+1) \) is true! Hence, by basically the same explanation as before (which fancy mathematicians call the "principle of induction"), \( P(n) \) is true for \( n = 1,2,3,4,\dots\).
