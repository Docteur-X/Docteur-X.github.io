The Technique called
Completing the Square
to make \( \displaystyle \textcolor{orange}{a} x^2 + \textcolor{green}{b} x + \textcolor{gray}{c} = \textcolor{orange}{a} \left( x + \frac{ \textcolor{green}{b} }{2 \textcolor{orange}{a} } \right)^2 - \frac{ \textcolor{green}{b} ^ 2}{4 \textcolor{orange}{a} } + \textcolor{gray}{c} \)

You can check that \( \displaystyle ax^2 + bx + c = a \left( x + \frac{b}{2a} \right)^2 - \frac{b^2}{4a} + c \).

To be made inside a drop down: \[ \begin{align} a \left( x + \frac{b}{2a} \right)^2 &= a \left( x + \frac{b}{2a} \right) \left( x + \frac{b}{2a} \right) \\ &= a \left( x \cdot \left( x + \frac{b}{2a} \right) + \frac{b}{2a} \cdot \left( x + \frac{b}{2a} \right) \right) \\ &= a \left( x \cdot x + x \cdot \frac{b}{2a} + \frac{b}{2a} \cdot x + \frac{b}{2a} \cdot \frac{b}{2a} \right) \\ &= a \left( x^2 + \frac{bx}{2a} + \frac{bx}{2a} + \frac{b \cdot b}{2a \cdot 2a} \right) \\ &= a \left( x^2 + \frac{bx}{a} + \frac{b^2}{4a^2} \right) \\ &= a x^2 + bx + \frac{b^2}{4a}\\ \end{align} \]

So \[ \begin{align} a \left( x + \frac{b}{2a} \right)^2 - \frac{b^2}{4a} + c &= a x^2 + bx + \frac{b^2}{4a} - \frac{b^2}{4a} + c \\ &= ax^2 + bx + c \end{align} \]

For example, if you want to turn \( \textcolor{red}{3}x^2 + \textcolor{orange}{1} x + (\textcolor{green}{-2}) \) into an expression that looks more like \( \textcolor{indigo}{p} ( x + \textcolor{brown}{q} )^2 + \textcolor{purple}{k} \), we use the above formula.

\[ \begin{align} \textcolor{red}{3}x^2 + \textcolor{orange}{1} \cdot x + (\textcolor{green}{-2}) &= \textcolor{red}{a}x^2 + \textcolor{orange}{b} x + \textcolor{green}{c} \\ &= \textcolor{red}{a}\left(x+\frac{\textcolor{orange}{b}}{2\textcolor{red}{a}}\right)^2 - \frac{\textcolor{orange}{b}^2}{4\textcolor{red}{a}} + \textcolor{green}{c} \\ &= \textcolor{red}{3}\left(x+\frac{\textcolor{orange}{1}}{2 \cdot \textcolor{red}{3}}\right)^2 - \frac{\textcolor{orange}{1}^2}{4 \cdot \textcolor{red}{3}} + (\textcolor{green}{-2}) \\ &= 3 \left(x+\frac{1}{6}\right)^2 - \frac{25}{12} \\ \end {align} \]

This is how I do it.

We start with a quadratic expression \( a x^2 + b x + \textcolor{gray}{c} \). We'd like to turn it into something like \( p (x + q)^2 + k \).

1. [[This paragraph may be too distracting. Include or not?]] The \( + \textcolor{gray}{c} \) bit can just go directly to the \( + k \). It won't change during the process of completing the square. This bulletpoint is just meta to tell you that you don't need to keep checking whether something is happening to \(+ \textcolor{gray}{c} \). Now you have more mental capacity to pay attention to the terms that will actually change, which is the \( x \) term and the \( x^2 \) term. But anyways.

2. Factor out \( a \) from the first two terms. \[ \textcolor{orange}{a} ( x^2 + \frac{\textcolor{green}{b}}{\textcolor{orange}{a}} x ) + \textcolor{gray}{c} \]

3. Now pay attention to the insides \( \displaystyle x^2 + \frac{\textcolor{green}{b}}{\textcolor{orange}{a}} x \).
