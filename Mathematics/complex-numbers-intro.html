What are Complex Numbers?

We pretend that there is a number called \( i \) so that \( i^2 = i \cdot i = -1 \). No real number has such a property, so you may argue that if \(i\) couldn't be a real number, it doesn't exist. Let's pretend it does exist; so \( i \neq 2, i \neq 0, i \neq -4.5, i \neq \pi , \) or any real number. There are 4 things you can do with complex numbers: you can add 2 complex numbers together, you can subtract one complex number from another, you can multiply complex numbers together, and you can divide a (possibly zero) complex number by another (nonzero) number.

We pretend it acts like a number that isn't zero; you can add it to another number, subtract it, multiply it with another number, and divide it with another number (you can't divide by 0).

The rules are exactly the same as the real numbers you're familiar with. Any complex number can be written like this: (real number)+(real number)\( \cdot i\). The only extra rule in complex numbers is \( i^2 = i \cdot i = -1 \).
