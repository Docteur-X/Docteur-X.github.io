Note before we begin.

You may have heard of \(i = \sqrt{-1}\). Later you'll see that one should actually write \( \sqrt{-1} = \pm i \) instead. We'll see that taking square roots and cube roots tend to create multiple values, while most other ordinary operations are much nicer, inputing just one value outputs one unique value, so forget about square roots for now. I'll show you how to understand complex numbers in a clearer way.

What are Complex Numbers?

Any complex number can be written like this: (real number)+(real number)\( \cdot i\). We pretend that there is a number called \( i \). It has the special property that \( i^2 = i \cdot i = -1 \); no real number has such a property, and you may argue it doesn't exist, but let's pretend it does exist. We pretend it acts like a number that isn't zero; you can add it to another number, subtract it, multiply it with another number, and divide it with another number (you can't divide by 0). Common sense also tells you that \( i \neq 0, i \neq 1, i\neq \text{any real number} \). There are 4 things you can do with complex numbers: you can add 2 complex numbers together, you can subtract one complex number from another, you can multiply complex numbers together, and you can divide a (possibly zero) complex number by another (nonzero) number.

The rules are exactly the same as the real numbers you're familiar with. The only extra rule in complex numbers is \( i^2 = i \cdot i = -1 \).
