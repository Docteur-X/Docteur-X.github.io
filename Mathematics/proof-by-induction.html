Proof by Induction

Someone made a bold claim. For any value of n, they say that the below statement is correct.

\[ \sum_{r=1}^n r^3 = \frac{1}{4}n^2(n+1)^2 \]

(In a clickable dropdown for the sum) \[ \sum_{r=1}^n r^3 \] means \[ 1^3 + 2^3 + 3^3 + \dots + n^3 \]. Add ref link to topic on summation.

Is the statement correct for \( \textcolor{orange}{n=1} \)? Let's check.

LHS: \( \sum_{r=1}^{\textcolor{orange}{n}} r^3 = \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{orange}{1} r^3 = 1^3 = 1 \)

RHS: \( \frac{1}{4}\textcolor{orange}{n}^2(\textcolor{orange}{n}+1)^2 = \frac{1}{4} \cdot \textcolor{orange}{1}^2 \cdot (\textcolor{orange}{1}+1)^2 = \frac{1}{4} \cdot 1 \cdot 2^2 = \frac{1}{4} \cdot 4 = 1 \)

The statement is correct for n=1. Is it correct for n=2? Let's check.

So the statement is correct for n=2.

We can also check that the statement is correct for n=3. And to check if the statement is correct for n=4, we again have to substitute n=4 into the equation and see if the left-hand side equals the right-hand side. We might enlist some slaves to help us tediously check if the statement is true for n=5, n=6, n=7, ... or we can use some clever method.

A Better Way

I've shown that the statement is true for n=2. Let's suppose I don't yet know if the statement is correct for n=3. Can I use the statement at n=2 to show that it is true at n=3?

\[ \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{green}{3} r^3 \overset{?}{=} \frac{1}{4} \cdot \textcolor{green}{3}^2 \cdot (\textcolor{green}{3}+1)^2 \]

LHS: \[ \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{green}{3} r^3 = \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{orange}{2} r^3 + \textcolor{green}{3}^3 = \frac{1}{4}\textcolor{orange}{2}^2(\textcolor{orange}{2}+1)^2 + \textcolor{green}{3}^3 \]

RHS: \[ \frac{1}{4} \cdot \textcolor{green}{3}^2 \cdot (\textcolor{green}{3}+1)^2 = \frac{1}{4} ( \textcolor{orange}{2} + 1 )^2 ( \textcolor{orange}{2} + 2 )^2 = \frac{1}{4} ( \textcolor{orange}{2} + 1 )^2 ( \textcolor{orange}{2}^2 + 2 \cdot 2 \cdot \textcolor{orange}{2} + 2^2 ) = \frac{1}{4} ( \textcolor{orange}{2} + 1 )^2 \textcolor{orange}{2}^2 + \frac{1}{4} ( \textcolor{orange}{2} + 1 )^2 \cdot (2 \cdot 2 \cdot \textcolor{orange}{2} + 2^2) = \frac{1}{4} ( \textcolor{orange}{2} + 1 )^2 \textcolor{orange}{2}^2 + \frac{1}{4} ( \textcolor{orange}{2} + 1 )^2 \cdot (2 \cdot 2 \cdot \textcolor{orange}{2} + 2^2) = \frac{1}{4} \textcolor{orange}{2}^2 ( \textcolor{orange}{2} + 1 )^2 + \textcolor{green}{3}^3 \]

Thus, the statement is correct for n=3. We've used the fact that the statement is true for n=2.

So, is the statement correct for n=4?

\[ \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{red}{4} r^3 \overset{?}{=} \frac{1}{4} \cdot \textcolor{red}{4}^2 \cdot (\textcolor{red}{4}+1)^2 \]

LHS: \[ \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{red}{4} r^3 = \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{green}{3} r^3 + \textcolor{red}{4}^3 = \frac{1}{4}\textcolor{green}{3}^2(\textcolor{green}{3}+1)^2 + \textcolor{red}{4}^3 \]

RHS: \[ \frac{1}{4} \cdot \textcolor{red}{4}^2 \cdot (\textcolor{red}{4}+1)^2 = \frac{1}{4} ( \textcolor{green}{3} + 1 )^2 ( \textcolor{green}{3} + 2 )^2 = \frac{1}{4} ( \textcolor{green}{3} + 1 )^2 ( \textcolor{green}{3}^2 + 2 \cdot 2 \cdot \textcolor{green}{3} + 2^2 ) = \frac{1}{4} ( \textcolor{green}{3} + 1 )^2 \textcolor{green}{3}^2 + \frac{1}{4} ( \textcolor{green}{3} + 1 )^2 \cdot (2 \cdot 2 \cdot \textcolor{green}{3} + 2^2) = \frac{1}{4} ( \textcolor{green}{3} + 1 )^2 \textcolor{green}{3}^2 + \frac{1}{4} ( \textcolor{green}{3} + 1 )^2 \cdot (2 \cdot 2 \cdot \textcolor{green}{3} + 2^2) = \frac{1}{4} \textcolor{green}{3}^2 ( \textcolor{green}{3} + 1 )^2 + \textcolor{red}{4}^3 \]

Thus the statement is correct for n=4.

Hopefully you see the trick here. You know that the statement is correct for n=1,2,3,4. Then by doing the following algebra:

\[ \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{red}{n+1} r^3 = \frac{1}{4}n^2(n+1)^2 + (n+1)^3 = \frac{1}{4}(n+1)^2(n+2)^2 \]

If the statement is correct for some n, the statement is correct for n+1.

So, because the statement is correct for n=4, it is correct for n=4+1=5, and correct for n=5+1=6, and correct for n=6+1=7, and correct for n=8, and n=9, and ...
