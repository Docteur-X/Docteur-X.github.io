Proof by Induction

Method

One has a statement \( \textcolor{purple}{P}(n) \) that is to be checked if it is true at \(n=\textcolor{blue}{1},\textcolor{orange}{2},\textcolor{green}{3},\dots\)

To do this, first check that \(\textcolor{purple}{P}(\textcolor{blue}{1})\) is true.

Then, by using a placeholder letter/variable \( k \) that can stand for any positive whole number, show that if \( \textcolor{purple}{P}(k) \) is true, then \( \textcolor{purple}{P}(k+1) \) is true for all \( k \in \mathcal{Z} \).

So let \( k=1 \), and if \( P(1) \) is true (which it is), then \( P(2) \) is true. Let k=2, so if \( P(2) \) is true, then \( P(3) \) is true. Let k=3, and if \( P(3) \) is true, so is \( P(4) \). If we kept on going, we get that \( P(n) \) is true for n=1,2,3,4... which is what we want to check for.

Examples to Convince You that it Works

Someone made a bold claim. For any value of n, they say that the below statement is correct.

\[ \sum_{r=1}^n r^3 = \frac{1}{4}n^2(n+1)^2 \]

(In a clickable dropdown for the sum) \[ \sum_{r=1}^n r^3 \] means \[ 1^3 + 2^3 + 3^3 + \dots + n^3 \]. Add ref link to topic on summation.

Is the statement correct for \( \textcolor{blue}{n=1} \)? Let's check.

LHS: \( \displaystyle \sum_{r=1}^{\textcolor{blue}{n}} r^3 = \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{blue}{1} r^3 = 1^3 = 1 \)

RHS: \( \displaystyle \frac{1}{4}\textcolor{blue}{n}^2(\textcolor{blue}{n}+1)^2 = \frac{1}{4} \cdot \textcolor{blue}{1}^2 \cdot (\textcolor{blue}{1}+1)^2 = \frac{1}{4} \cdot 1 \cdot 2^2 = \frac{1}{4} \cdot 4 = 1 \)

The statement is correct for \( \textcolor{blue}{n=1} \). Is it correct for \( \textcolor{orange}{n=2} \)? Let's check.

LHS: \( \displaystyle \sum_{r=1}^{\textcolor{orange}{n}} r^3 = \sum_{r=2}^\textcolor{orange}{1} r^3 = 1^3 + 2^3 = 1 + 8 = 9 \)

RHS: \( \displaystyle \frac{1}{4}\textcolor{orange}{n}^2(\textcolor{orange}{n}+1)^2 = \frac{1}{4} \cdot \textcolor{orange}{2}^2 \cdot (\textcolor{orange}{2}+1)^2 = \frac{1}{4} \cdot 2^2 \cdot 3^2 = \frac{1}{4} \cdot 4 \cdot 9 = 9 \)

So the statement is correct for \( \textcolor{orange}{n=2} \).

We can also check that the statement is correct for \textcolor{green}{n=3}. And to check if the statement is correct for \textcolor{red}{n=4}, we again substitute \textcolor{red}{n=4} into the equation and see if the left-hand side equals the right-hand side. We might enlist some slaves to help us tediously check if the statement is true for n=5, n=6, n=7, ... or we can use some cleverer method.

A Better Way, which is why we learn Proof by Induction

I've shown that the statement \( \displaystyle \sum_{r=1}^n r^3 = \frac{1}{4}n^2(n+1)^2\) is true when \( \textcolor{orange}{n=2} \). I don't yet know if the statement is correct for \( \textcolor{green}{n=3} = \textcolor{orange}{2} + 1 \). Can I use the statement at \( \textcolor{orange}{n=2} \) to show that the statement is true at \( \textcolor{green}{n=3} \)?

\[ \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{green}{3} r^3 \overset{?}{=} \frac{1}{4} \cdot \textcolor{green}{3}^2 \cdot (\textcolor{green}{3}+1)^2 \]

LHS: \[ \begin{aligned} \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{green}{3} r^3 &= \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{orange}{2} r^3 + (\textcolor{green}{3})^3 & \textcolor{gray}{\text{Split off the last term from the summation}} \\ &= \frac{1}{4} \cdot \textcolor{orange}{2}^2\cdot(\textcolor{orange}{2}+1)^2 + (\textcolor{orange}{2}+1)^3 & \textcolor{gray}{\text{3 = 2+1}} \\ &= \frac{1}{4} (\textcolor{orange}{2}+1)^2 ( \textcolor{orange}{2}^2 + 4(\textcolor{orange}{2}+1)) & \textcolor{gray}{\text{Factor out} \frac{1}{4}(2+1)^2)} \\ &= \frac{1}{4} (\textcolor{orange}{2}+1)^2 ( \textcolor{orange}{2}^2 + 4\textcolor{orange}{2}+4)) & \textcolor{gray}{\text{Expand out 4(\textcolor{orange}{2}+1)}} \\ &= \frac{1}{4} (\textcolor{orange}{2}+1)^2(\textcolor{orange}{2}+2)^2 & \textcolor{gray}{\text{Use the fact that} (x+2)^2 = x^2 + 4x + 4 } \\ &= \frac{1}{4} (\textcolor{orange}{2}+1)^2( (\textcolor{orange}{2}+1) +1)^2 & \textcolor{gray}{\text{}} \\ &= \frac{1}{4} (\textcolor{green}{3})^2(\textcolor{green}{3} + 1)^2 & \textcolor{gray}{3 = 2+1} \end{aligned} \]

LHS: \[ \begin{aligned} \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{green}{3} r^3 &= \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{orange}{2} r^3 + \textcolor{green}{3}^3 \\ &= \frac{1}{4}\textcolor{orange}{2}^2(\textcolor{orange}{2}+1)^2 + \textcolor{green}{3}^3 \end{aligned} \]

RHS: \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{1}{4} \cdot \textcolor{green}{3}^2 \cdot (\textcolor{green}{3}+1)^2 &= \frac{1}{4} ( \textcolor{orange}{2} + 1 )^2 ( (\textcolor{orange}{2} + 1) + 1 )^2 \\ &= \frac{1}{4} ( \textcolor{orange}{2} + 1 )^2 ( \textcolor{orange}{2} + 2 )^2 \\ &= \frac{1}{4} ( \textcolor{orange}{2} + 1 )^2 ( \textcolor{orange}{2}^2 + 2 \cdot 2 \cdot \textcolor{orange}{2} + 2^2 ) \\ &= \frac{1}{4} ( \textcolor{orange}{2} + 1 )^2 ( \textcolor{orange}{2}^2 + 4 \cdot \textcolor{orange}{2} + 4 ) \\ &= \frac{1}{4} ( \textcolor{orange}{2} + 1 )^2 \textcolor{orange}{2}^2 + \cancel{\frac{1}{4}} ( \textcolor{orange}{2} + 1 )^2 \cdot (\cancel{4} \cdot \textcolor{orange}{2} + \cancelto{4}{1}) \\ &= \frac{1}{4} ( \textcolor{orange}{2} + 1 )^2 \textcolor{orange}{2}^2 + ( \textcolor{orange}{2} + 1 )^2 \cdot (\textcolor{orange}{2}) \\ &= \frac{1}{4} ( \textcolor{orange}{2} + 1 )^2 \textcolor{orange}{2}^2 + ( \textcolor{orange}{2} + 1 )^3 \\ &= \frac{1}{4} \textcolor{orange}{2}^2 ( \textcolor{orange}{2} + 1 )^2 + \textcolor{green}{3}^3 \end{aligned} \]

Thus, the statement is correct for \( \textcolor{green}{n=3} \). We've used the fact that the statement is true for \( \textcolor{orange}{n=2} \).

So, is the statement correct for \( \textcolor{red}{4} \)?

\[ \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{red}{4} r^3 \overset{?}{=} \frac{1}{4} \cdot \textcolor{red}{4}^2 \cdot (\textcolor{red}{4}+1)^2 \]

LHS: \[ \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{red}{4} r^3 = \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{green}{3} r^3 + \textcolor{red}{4}^3 = \frac{1}{4}\textcolor{green}{3}^2(\textcolor{green}{3}+1)^2 + \textcolor{red}{4}^3 \]

RHS: \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{1}{4} \cdot \textcolor{red}{4}^2 \cdot (\textcolor{red}{4}+1)^2 &= \frac{1}{4} ( \textcolor{green}{3} + 1 )^2 ( (\textcolor{green}{3} + 1) + 1 )^2 \\ &= \frac{1}{4} ( \textcolor{green}{3} + 1 )^2 ( \textcolor{green}{3} + 2 )^2 \\ &= \frac{1}{4} ( \textcolor{green}{3} + 1 )^2 ( \textcolor{green}{3}^2 + 2 \cdot 2 \cdot \textcolor{green}{3} + 2^2 ) \\ &= \frac{1}{4} ( \textcolor{green}{3} + 1 )^2 ( \textcolor{green}{3}^2 + 4 \cdot \textcolor{green}{3} + 4 ) \\ &= \frac{1}{4} ( \textcolor{green}{3} + 1 )^2 \textcolor{green}{3}^2 + \cancel{\frac{1}{4}} ( \textcolor{green}{3} + 1 )^2 \cdot (\cancel{4} \cdot \textcolor{green}{3} + \cancelto{4}{1}) \\ &= \frac{1}{4} ( \textcolor{green}{3} + 1 )^2 \textcolor{green}{3}^2 + ( \textcolor{green}{3} + 1 )^2 \cdot (\textcolor{green}{3}) \\ &= \frac{1}{4} ( \textcolor{green}{3} + 1 )^2 \textcolor{green}{3}^2 + ( \textcolor{green}{3} + 1 )^3 \\ &= \frac{1}{4} \textcolor{green}{3}^2 ( \textcolor{green}{3} + 1 )^2 + \textcolor{red}{4}^3 \end{aligned} \]

Thus the statement is correct for \( \textcolor{red}{4} \).

Hopefully you see the trick here. You know that the statement is correct for \( n = \textcolor{blue}{1},\textcolor{orange}{2},\textcolor{green}{3},\textcolor{red}{4} \). Then do the following algebra:

\[ \begin{aligned} \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{purple}{n+1} r^3 &= \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{brown}{n} r^3 + (\textcolor{purple}{n+1})^3 \\ &= \frac{1}{4} \textcolor{brown}{n}^2(\textcolor{brown}{n}+1)^2 + (\textcolor{brown}{n}+1)^3 \\ &= \frac{1}{4} (\textcolor{brown}{n}+1)^2 ( \textcolor{brown}{n}^2 + 4(\textcolor{brown}{n}+1)) \\ &= \frac{1}{4} (\textcolor{brown}{n}+1)^2 ( \textcolor{brown}{n}^2 + 4\textcolor{brown}{n}+4)) \\ &= \frac{1}{4}(\textcolor{brown}{n}+1)^2(\textcolor{brown}{n}+2)^2\\ &= \frac{1}{4}(\textcolor{purple}{n+1})^2(\textcolor{purple}{n+1} + 1)^2 \end{aligned} \]

If the statement is correct for some \( \textcolor{brown}{n} \), the statement is correct for \( \textcolor{purple}{n+1} \).

So, because the statement is correct for n=4, it is correct for n=4+1=5, and correct for n=5+1=6, and correct for n=6+1=7, and correct for n=8, and n=9, and ...

So the statement is correct for every positive integer.

To recap, we need to check that the statement is correct for \( \textcolor{blue}{n=1}\). Then the statement is correct if n=1. Use algebra to show that if the statement is true for n=1, the statement is also true for n+1=2. The same algebra says that if the statement is true for n=2, the statement is also true for n+1=3. And if the statement is true for n=3, it's true for n=4, and then n=5, and n=6, and ... This means that the statement is true for n=1,2,3,4,... which is what we want to show.

Another Summation Example

Someone else made another bold claim. They claim that the following statement is true for every positive integer n:

\[ \sum_{r=1}^n r(r+1) = \frac{n(n+1)(n+2)}{3} \]

Let's apply the above procedure.

Step 1. Check the statement is true for \( \textcolor{blue}{n=1} \).

LHS: \( \displaystyle \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{blue}{n} r(r+1) = \sum_{r=1}^\textcolor{blue}{1} r(r+1) = \textcolor{blue}{1} \cdot (\textcolor{blue}{1} + 1) = 1 \cdot 2 = 2\)

RHS: \( \displaystyle \frac{\textcolor{blue}{n}(\textcolor{blue}{n}+1)(\textcolor{blue}{n}+2)}{3} = \frac{\textcolor{blue}{1}(\textcolor{blue}{1}+1)(\textcolor{blue}{1}+2)}{3} = \frac{1\cdot 2\cdot \cancel{3}}{\cancel{3}} = 2 \)

Thus the statement is true for \( \textcolor{blue}{n=1}\).

Step 2, assume that \( \displaystyle \sum_{r=1}^n r(r+1) = \frac{n(n+1)(n+2)}{3} \) for some n. We know it is true for n=1; it might be true for other values of n as well. We'd like to show that \( \displaystyle \sum_{r=1}^{n+1} r(r+1) = \frac{(n+1)(n+2)(n+3)}{3} \).

\[ \begin{aligned} \sum_{r=1}^{n+1} r(r+1) &= \sum_{r=1}^n r(r+1) + (n+1)((n+1)+1)\\ &= \frac{n(n+1)(n+2)}{3} + (n+1)(n+2) \\ &= \frac{(n+1)(n+2)}{3} \cdot (n + 3) \\ &= \frac{(n+1)(n+2)(n+3)}{3} \end{aligned} \]

Thus, the statement is true for n=1, and is true for n+1=2, and true for n=3, and 4, and ...

Proof of a statement related to Divisibility using Induction

Another person made another bold claim:

The value of \( \displaystyle 3^{2n} + 11 \) calculated at any (positive integer) n is a whole number that is divisible by 4.

(Dropdown: If you have learned modular arithmetic, there is an easy proof.) \[ 3^{2n} + 11 \pmod{4} = (-1)^{2n} + 11 - 12 \pmod{4} = 1 + 11 - 12 \pmod{4} = 0 \pmod{4} \]

Step 1. Check the statement is true for \( \textcolor{blue}{n=1}\).

\[ \displaystyle 3^{2\textcolor{blue}{n}} + 11 = 3^{2 \textcolor{blue}{1}} + 11 = 3^2 + 11 = 9 + 11 = 20 = 4 \cdot 5\]

Thus, \( \displaystyle 3^{2n} + 11\) is divisible by 4 when \( \textcolor{blue}{n=1}\). So the statement is true for \( \textcolor{blue}{n=1}\)
