1. The Physics Admissions Test (PAT), the Oxford entrance exam for physics and engineering, was recently scrapped this January 2027. From hearsay in the Physics Department, the physics professors were asked if they think the PAT should cost money to sit; students are already paying like 50 quid-ish in administrative fees that goes to the location they sit for the exam, and Oxford doesn't even get any of that money. Despite Oxford charging nothing for sitting the exam, I actually liked the exam style; it has 10 MCQ questions which will give you partial marks if you did the right working even if you get the wrong answer, and a good amount of open ended long-form questions. Every respectable physicist at Oxford thinks it does a good and decently fair job of distinguishing between top candidates.

Anyways, pretty much all the physics profs opposed the idea of secondary school students paying for the PAT, so you know what the Uni (I think it's the Admissions Office here) decided to do? If you don't want to pay for the PAT, then scrap the PAT, and outsource it to someone else (UAT in this case). Starting from 2027/2028, students now sit the ESAT. The ESAT is just a time-pressured MCQ, no partial marks, no long-form questions, and costs 78 quid for UK students. So despite being more expensive, it's literally an ABC choice that a human paid on minimum wage will shove into an OCR machine, no long-form questions to test your deep and solid understanding of physics, and no professor is going to look at your working and give you partial marks. Now it's going to be much harder to distinguish between the deserving top candidates because it's just an MCQ.

2. There's asbestos in the brutalist concrete uglyness of the Denys Wilkinson Building (DWB). Apparently the physics department kind of knew about it for weeks, but there was no communication with us students. It was some teaching lab demonstrators (probably some poor PhD students) finding out recently and then the Cherwell newspaper investigating to find out that the ceilings actually do have asbestos. It's so infuriating because the undergraduate physicists been doing our labs in there throughout the whole year.

Undergraduates do their labs in the basement of the DWB, so you'd descend some gritty black rocky stairs that gets wet when it rains, and I remember someone with a cast on their leg slipping at the bottom of these stairs and landing on their hands; genuinely could have been worst. The design, lighting and dustiness inside is actually ok, apart from the fact that it is a proper basement with no natural lighting or windows outside of the foyer area; even poor Jenny Barnes' office has just some thin longitudinal windows looking out into those disgusting stairs.

The real trouble comes from booking labs you want. You literally have to book for labs the hour they become live on SPire if you want to get the labs you want.

3. Every year there'll be the exact same complaints to the PJCC, the student representation for the central physics teaching office. If you want to make changes, be very very ready for bureaucracy.

(a) "Our tutorials don't match up with the lectures." Ask your tutors in college.

(b) "We don't get past paper solutions." We don't want students to think that there is only one way of doing the paper.

(c) "Can we please get lecture recordings for XXX"
